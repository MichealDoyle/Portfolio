Color Theory

Overview

Color Theory is the understanding of how different colors interact with each other and how they can be used to provide contrast, harmony, and even provoke emotions. Below is a list of key color theory terms that help give us a better understanding of what principles make up color theory.

Color Theory Terms

Color Wheel
The color wheel is a fundamental tool in color theory, illustrating the relationship between colors.
Primary Colors
Primary colors, which are pure and cannot be created by mixing other colors, include, Red, Blue, and Yellow.
Secondary Colors
Secondary colors are formed by mixing two primary colors. Green, Violet, and Orange are the secondary colors.
Tertiary Colors
Tertiary colors result from mixing a primary color with a secondary color. There are six possible tertiary colors.
Saturation
Saturation refers to the intensity and purity of a color.
Hues
Hues are often defined as the purest form of a color. There are twelve hues that are considered to be the purest and most vivid. The three primary colors, the three secondary colors, and the six tertiary colors.
Shades
Shades of a hue are made by mixing the hue with black.
Tints
Tints of a hue are made by mixing the hue with white.
Tones
Tones of a hue are made by mixing the hue with gray.
Value of the Hue
The value of the hue refers to its relative lightness or darkness. When values are close, elements will appear to blend together. When values differ significantly, creating a greater contrast, the most distinct areas of the image will stand out or "pop".
Neutral Colors
Neutral colors are often perceived as the absence of color, but they usually contain subtle undertones.
Warm Colors
Warm bring energy and intensity to a design. They often evoke strong emotions and can create a sense of excitement or warmth.
Cool Colors
Cool colors contribute to a calm and soothing atmosphere. These colors are associated with relaxation and can evoke a peaceful, tranquil feeling.
Monochromatic Colors
A monochromatic color scheme consist of a single hue with variations in shades, tints and tones. this approach creates a minimal yet impactful design, directing attention to key elements.
Color Scheme
A color scheme is a defined set of colors that ensures a cohesive look and feel across a project.
Triadic Colors
Triadic colors are a group of three colors that are evenly spaced on the color wheel. A common color scheme known for its vibrant and harmonious feel, as it balances the use of colors while maintaining contrast and richness.
Complementary colors
Complementary colors are pairs of colors that when combined, cancel out by producing a grayscale color like white or black. When placed next to each other, they create the strongest contrast and reinforce each other. On the traditional color wheel, complementary colors are placed directly across from each other.
Split Colors
Split colors involves using a variation of color families to create visual interest and harmony. This technique focuses on splitting complementary colors by incorporating analogous hues, which helps generate a more dynamic and visually appealing composition.
Analogous Colors
Analogous colors refers to groups of colors that are next to each other on the color wheel. These colors usually match well together and create serene and comfortable designs. they are often found in nature and are harmonious and pleasing to the eye.
Contrasting
In color theory, contrasting refers to the impact of combining hues in a way that highlights their differences, thus enhancing their visual impact. Contrasting colors are often used to make each other stand out, creating a dynamic and visually appealing effect.
Vibrancy
In color theory, vibrancy refers to the brightness, intensity, and energy of a hue. Vibrant colors naturally draw attention and create a sense of excitement, movement, or warmth, depending on their placement and context.
Whitespace
Empty space is often referred to as whitespace. Whitespace is a powerful tool that can be used to direct attention, create balance, and establish a visual hierarchy.
