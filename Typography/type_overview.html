Overview

Typography is more than just selecting any font for a project; it includes understanding the difference between typefaces and fonts, where and how to place the text, and feelings certain fonts and typefaces provide. Proper typography does more than make text look attractive, it ensures clarity, readability, and effective communication throughout your designs and content. Below is a list of key typography terms to help understand the anatomy of typography.

Ascender Line:
The guideline that marks the uppermost point of an ascender, which is the part of a lowercase letter extending above the x-height.
Cap Height:
The height of an uppercase letter from the baseline to the top of the letter.
X-Height:
The height of lowercase letter typically measured by lowercase x. This measurement varies across the typefaces and significantly affects readability in body text.
Baseline:
The invisible line on which most characters sit, providing consistency in alignment.
Descender Line:
The guideline indicating the lowest point of a descender, which is the portion of a lowercase letter that extends below the baseline.
Serif:
Small projections or "feet" at the end of the strokes in certain typefaces. Serif fonts enhance readability in body text.
Bracket:
The curved or angled connection between a serif and the main stroke of a letter. The shape and contrast of the Bracket contribute to a typefaces style.
Terminal:
The end of a stroke that does not have a serif. This can be rounded, flat, or angled, depending on the typeface.
Loop:
A fully closed or partially closed counter at the bottom of certain lowercase letters.
Stem:
The primary vertical or diagonal stroke of a letterform, providing structural support.
Arm:
A horizontal or upward-slanting stroke that does not connect to another stroke at one end.
